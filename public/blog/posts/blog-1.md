The world of data science is filled with brilliant minds building incredibly sophisticated models.

We celebrate the elegance of new algorithms and the predictive power of machine learning. Yet, a common point of failure for data science initiatives in the corporate world isn't the quality of the model, but its collision with operational reality. An elegant model that cannot be implemented, understood, or trusted by the team on the ground is little more than an academic exercise.

I experienced this firsthand when I took on the directorship at A M Foods, managing a restaurant brand, Ajanabi, with a 50-year legacy. The problem was clear: significant food waste and inefficient inventory management. A typical data science approach might have been to pull historical sales data and build a complex forecasting model. But the real problem wasn't a lack of data; it was the lack of a system to capture and act on it. The business ran on manual notes and institutional memory.

Instead of starting with the algorithm, we started with the operation. Started simple using spreadsheets. The challenge was to create a system that was intuitive for the kitchen and management staff. We knew spreadsheets would reach a point where it will be cumbersome to manage. We used this time to architect and built a custom software platform for inventory and payroll that solved their immediate, tangible problems. The data we needed was a product of this new operational workflow.

This is the crucial, often-missed link. The most impactful data science isn't performed in a vacuum. It's about deeply understanding the business process and then building solutions—whether they are simple dashboards or complex ML models—that fit seamlessly into that context. As data leaders, our first question shouldn't be 'What can the data tell us?' but rather, 'What is the operational problem we need to solve?'
